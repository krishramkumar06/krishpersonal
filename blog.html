Here's a very nice problem that was for extra credit for my most recent math quiz. We were being assessed on convergence tests for infinite series. The the question was to prove that \( \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{2^{\ln n}}{n^2} \). My first idea was that \( \sum \frac{e^{\ln n}}{n^2} = \sum \frac{1}{n}\) diverged. I wanted to use limit comparison with this, but it turn out the solution is much more straightforward. With the same logic, \(2^{\ln n} = ((e^{(\ln 2)}))^{\ln n} = n^{\ln 2}.\) Plugging this into our sum, we now have \( \sum \frac{n^{\ln 2}}{n^2} = \sum \frac{1}{n^{2 - \ln 2}} \). Since \(2 < e \rightarrow \ln 2 < 1 \rightarrow 2 - \ln 2> 1 \), this converges by the p-series test.
